# Paragraph & Sentence Rubric

## Paragraph score (0–5)

- **0** — No evident authorship concern.
- **1** — Low risk; conventional academic wording.
- **2** — Mild generic/formulaic features.
- **3** — Moderate AI-likeness or weak authorship signal; revision or verification recommended.
- **4** — High AI-likeness or strong authorship weakness.
- **5** — Severe authorship-risk profile requiring detailed author verification. **Rare.** Do not
  assign 5 to naturally formulaic methods/ethics text unless there are additional severe concerns
  (e.g. generic non-alignment with the actual study, or direct concordance with a
  detector-highlighted passage).

For each paragraph record: ID · section · score · confidence (low/medium/high) · main concern code
· evidence snippet (or concise paraphrase) · false-positive caution · **coached recommended
action** (what a stronger authorial version would do, explained so the student can do it).

## Section calibration

Apply **caution** (do not over-score) to naturally standardised sections: methods, ethics
statements, statistical analysis, inclusion/exclusion criteria, administrative declarations,
structured-abstract subheadings, reporting-guideline boilerplate.

Apply **stricter** expectations where the student's reasoning should be visible: introduction,
literature framing, rationale, discussion, interpretation of findings, implications for practice,
limitations, conclusion.

## Sentence concern codes (for paragraphs scored 3–5 or externally flagged)

- **GEN** — generic claim.
- **POL** — excessive polish/smoothness without substance.
- **UNS** — unsupported claim.
- **VAG** — vague transition or vague causal link.
- **REP** — repetitive sentence architecture.
- **CIT** — weak citation integration.
- **DAT** — weak link to data/results.
- **MET** — weak link to methods.
- **TER** — inconsistent terminology.
- **TRN** — translation-like phrasing.
- **PAR** — suspicious paraphrasing or homogenised rewriting.
- **STD** — standardised/formulaic section caution.

Sentence-level comments identify *local* authorship concerns; they never independently prove a
sentence is AI-generated.

## Coaching note

Every recommended action is phrased to teach: name the weakness, explain why it reads as weak
authorship, and describe what the student could add from their own knowledge of the study (specific
findings, local context, methodological reasoning) to strengthen it — **without** changing any
locked claim, number, or citation.
