# Section-Specific Guidance for Health-Science Texts

## Title

A strong title should identify the topic, population, setting, design or focus, and outcome when appropriate. Avoid overstated causality unless the design supports it.

## Abstract

Revise for accurate compression. Preserve objective, design, population, methods, results, and conclusion. Do not add results absent from the manuscript. Insert `[DATA REQUIRED]` when numerical findings are missing.

## Introduction / Background

Strengthen:

- burden or relevance of the issue;
- local, regional, or clinical context;
- specific population or practice setting;
- gap in knowledge or practice;
- logical transition to objective.

Avoid generic statements such as “health is very important” or “many studies have shown” without references.

## Literature Review

Move from descriptive listing to synthesis:

- compare objectives, populations, methods, tools, and findings;
- identify convergence and divergence;
- distinguish global evidence from Moroccan or local evidence;
- discuss limitations and gaps;
- preserve source traceability.

## Methods

Do not overinterpret formulaic wording as poor authorship. Strengthen operational clarity:

- design;
- setting;
- population;
- eligibility criteria;
- sampling;
- sample size rationale;
- instrument/tool;
- data collection timing;
- variables;
- analysis plan;
- ethics and consent.

Use markers when details are absent.

## Results

Results must reflect supplied data. Do not invent numbers, trends, p-values, themes, categories, or quotes. Use `[DATA REQUIRED]` when findings are referenced but not supplied.

## Discussion

Prioritize authorial reasoning:

- interpret the main findings;
- compare with literature;
- explain plausible mechanisms or contextual factors;
- discuss implications for practice, education, policy, or research;
- include limitations;
- avoid unsupported causal claims.

## Conclusion

Keep conclusions proportional to the study design and findings. Avoid broad statements not supported by the data. Include practical implications only if they follow from the results.

## References

Do not fabricate references. If a claim requires support and no source is supplied, use `[REFERENCE REQUIRED]`.

## Notes de cadrage

For ESSS-style notes de cadrage, preserve the institutional structure and strengthen:

- context;
- importance;
- review component;
- empirical component;
- ethical considerations;
- expected outputs;
- article-title proposals.

Do not transform a student-level project into an unrealistic doctoral-level protocol.
