# Scholarly Revision Framework

## Principle

Revise from substance to style. A paragraph should become stronger because it is more precise, evidence-linked, methodologically transparent, and authorially accountable — not because it merely sounds less AI-generated.

## Revision hierarchy

1. **Scientific accuracy** — remove ambiguity, overstatement, unsupported claims, and imprecise terminology.
2. **Evidence traceability** — link literature claims to sources, empirical claims to data, and methodological claims to actual procedures.
3. **Authorial specificity** — tie claims to the study setting, population, variables, instruments, and findings.
4. **Methodological transparency** — clarify design, sampling, data collection, analysis, ethics, validity, and limitations.
5. **Argument coherence** — align aim, methods, results, interpretation, implications, and conclusion.
6. **Disciplinary register** — use health-science terminology appropriate to the field and audience.
7. **Language quality** — improve grammar, clarity, flow, and readability without flattening the author’s meaning.

## Revision types

- **Clarifying revision:** improves readability without changing scientific content.
- **Substantive revision:** strengthens claims, logic, context, and interpretation.
- **Methodological revision:** clarifies design, population, instruments, variables, procedures, or analysis.
- **Evidence revision:** adds required references or marks missing citations.
- **Interpretive revision:** aligns discussion and conclusion with actual findings.
- **Structural revision:** reorganizes sections for academic coherence.

## Prohibited superficial fixes

Do not revise by:

- randomizing sentence length;
- adding colloquial expressions;
- adding “personal voice” unsupported by author evidence;
- using awkward wording to look human;
- replacing terms with less precise synonyms;
- reducing academic rigor to lower detector suspicion.
